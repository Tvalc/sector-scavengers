# Sector Scavengers LitRPG Series Bible

## Core direction

Turn `Sector Scavengers` into a system-isekai LitRPG where the protagonist does not merely play the salvage game. They wake up inside it.

The world is an orbital graveyard made of derelict ships, dead stations, cryo vaults, and salvage corridors. Every major survival rule is governed by the same stats and progression systems that already exist in the game. The system is real, visible, enforceable, and indifferent.

This gives the series three strong hooks at once:

1. Blue-collar survival in a hostile wreck field.
2. LitRPG progression with hard numbers and meaningful choices.
3. A larger mystery about who built the scavenger system and why living people are waking inside it.

## The bookable premise

The main character wakes in a cryo pod aboard the only functioning starter station in a dead orbital sector. A cold system voice identifies them as a newly activated scavenger and displays the same ship, crew, mission, and resource stats that govern the actual game.

They learn five terrifying truths fast:

1. The sector contains a fixed grid of sixteen drifting ships.
2. Most ships are derelicts that must be salvaged, repaired, and claimed under system rules.
3. Hull breaches, resource shortages, and failed dives kill for real.
4. More survivors can be awakened from cryo, but each activation costs rare power cells.
5. The only way out is up: build a fleet, convert wrecks into stations, master the system, and push into deeper sectors.

## How the game systems become diegetic story rules

Use the actual game numbers as the reality engine, not just inspiration.

### Sector structure

- Each starting sector contains `16` ships arranged as a `4 x 4` salvage grid.
- The protagonist begins with `1` starter ship already under player control.
- The starter ship is a station, which means it can hold rooms, crew, and long-term infrastructure.
- All other ships begin as derelicts until claimed or mined out.

### Ship stats on page

Every important ship should be readable in-universe through a system pane:

- `Ship Class`: `1-3`
- `Hull Integrity`: `0-100`
- `Owner`: Neutral or Player
- `Mode`: Derelict, Claimed, Mined, Station
- `Claim Progress`: tracked until claim threshold is reached
- `Power Accumulated`: passive operational energy
- `Room Slots`: unlocked after station conversion

That lets the setting feel like true LitRPG without inventing a second magic system.

### Core currencies

- `Energy/Power`: common operating currency for dives and actions.
- `Metal`: common construction resource.
- `Tech`: uncommon upgrade resource.
- `Components`: rare build and upgrade resource.
- `Power Cells`: premium progression resource used to wake crew and convert ships into stations.

Power cells should feel like the emotional center of progression. Spending one is never casual because it means choosing between waking a person and expanding infrastructure.

### Run loop in story form

Each major salvage operation can follow the same underlying logic as the game:

- A dive targets one ship.
- The system run lasts up to `10` rounds of escalating danger.
- The protagonist chooses between high-risk gain and safe extraction.
- Hull breach remains the core fail state.
- Surviving and extracting is often more important than greed.

This is what makes the series bingeable: every book can contain multiple tense self-contained salvage runs inside a larger political and survival arc.

### Claiming ships

Claiming a ship should be a serious narrative milestone, not just a bookkeeping event.

- A ship becomes claimable after `3` qualifying advances.
- Those advances can come from successful repairs, successful runs, or a mix of both.
- Final claiming costs energy and power cells, which makes the moment expensive and therefore dramatic.

That creates a clean fiction-first loop:

1. Survive the wreck.
2. Learn its secrets.
3. Repair enough of it to bind it.
4. Decide whether to strip it for resources or keep it as a permanent asset.

### Crew as real people, not just bonuses

Every awakened crew member is both a stat package and a relationship.

- `Engineer`: repair specialist, conversion enabler.
- `Scientist`: boosts discovery and research.
- `Medic`: keeps people alive and raises efficiency.
- `Scavenger`: improves salvage yield and extraction success.

Because waking crew costs power cells, every new companion should arrive with weight, debt, and consequences. Crew growth can mirror the actual progression system:

- Level `1-5`
- XP from assignments, run completion, and surviving danger
- Possible permanent death on catastrophic failure

That combination gives the series its emotional engine. You are not just optimizing a party. You are deciding who gets to wake up and who must keep sleeping in the dark.

### Stations and room building

Once a ship is converted into a station, the protagonist gets city-builder style progression inside the survival story.

Available room types already map cleanly to fiction:

- `Crew Quarters`: community, population ceiling, social tension.
- `Science Lab`: reverse-engineering alien salvage, discovery arcs.
- `Medical Bay`: trauma, recovery, lower death pressure.
- `Recreation Deck`: morale, culture, fragile normalcy.
- `Cargo Hold`: logistics, scarcity, convoy stakes.
- `Engineering Bay`: power generation, conversion, heavy industry.

This is where the series stops being only about surviving wrecks and starts becoming about building civilization.

### Mission ladder

The mission types also scale naturally as novel content:

- `Salvage Run`: quick tactical problem.
- `Sector Patrol`: local threats, scouts, pirates, drones.
- `Trade Convoy`: economics, diplomacy, betrayal.
- `Deep Space Survey`: high-risk frontier pushes with major revelations.

That gives each book a strong mix of dungeon-crawl tension and larger strategic movement.

## Recommended protagonist

### Working lead

Use a protagonist with technical instincts, blue-collar voice, and enough emotional damage to make the cryo wake-up matter.

Suggested baseline:

- Name: `Rook Vance`
- Pre-isekai identity: overworked salvage-tech or systems QA contractor
- Core flaw: treats people like systems because systems were easier than grief
- Core strength: sees patterns fast and keeps functioning under pressure
- Core wound: failed to save someone before the transfer into this world

Why this works:

- They can understand the stats without sounding like a lore dump.
- Their sarcasm can carry the tone.
- Their emotional arc pushes against the dehumanizing logic of the system.

## Tone target

Aim for:

- desperate but funny
- dangerous but not nihilistic
- crunchy but readable
- emotionally attached to crew
- full of ugly practical details: air, heat, scrap, blood, debt, sleep, oxygen, pressure doors

If you want a `Dungeon Crawler Carl` adjacent feel, chase:

- gallows humor
- escalating public spectacle
- absurd but lethal system logic
- deep loyalty between damaged people

Do not chase:

- nonstop quips with no grief
- clean heroic power fantasy
- vague stat windows that never change outcomes

## What the system should look like on page

Use short, sharp status panes. Example:

`[SCAVENGER INTERFACE]`
`Sector: Graveyard 7`
`Controlled Ships: 1/16`
`Energy: 150`
`Resources: Metal 0 | Tech 0 | Components 0 | Power Cells 0`
`Active Crew: 0`
`Available Cryo Pods: 2`
`Current Objective: Claim 1 additional ship`

For ship reads:

`[SHIP 09 - DERELICT]`
`Class: 2`
`Hull Integrity: 46/100`
`Claim Progress: 1/3`
`Risk: Elevated`
`Recommended Crew: Engineer, Scavenger`

For crew:

`[CREW - ECHO CHEN]`
`Role: Engineer`
`Level: 2`
`Efficiency: 71`
`Technical: 84`
`Luck: 48`
`Speed: 52`
`Assignment: Ship 09`

Keep the boxes short. The narration should carry emotion; the system panes should carry clarity.

## Seven-book series arc

Use a seven-book first arc because the current published `Dungeon Crawler Carl` run provides seven usable length comps.

### Book 1 - Graveyard Wake

Pitch:
Rook wakes alone on the starter station, learns the rules of the salvage grid, survives the first hull breaches, and realizes the cryo pods contain real people who can only be awakened by power cells earned through lethal runs.

Book goal:
- establish the sector
- teach the reader the system cleanly
- claim the first new ship
- wake the first crew member
- reveal that the system is older and crueler than it looks

Ending image:
Rook stands on two claimed ships instead of one and understands that staying small means dying later.

### Book 2 - Cold Claim

Pitch:
With a tiny crew and fragile foothold, Rook starts converting wrecks into stations, runs patrol and trade missions, and collides with other awakened captains who obey the same system but not the same ethics.

Book goal:
- expand from survival to strategy
- introduce rival scavenger factions
- wake two to three key crew members
- force a painful power-cell choice between infrastructure and people

Ending image:
Rook wins a ship war but learns the sector itself is only a tutorial cage.

### Book 3 - The Wreck Choir

Pitch:
Deep surveys uncover ships whose logs suggest thousands of scavengers have risen and vanished before. The protagonist must descend into more dangerous class-2 and class-3 wrecks while a growing crew starts to become a real society.

Book goal:
- deepen the mystery
- increase station complexity through rooms and specialization
- show the emotional cost of command
- introduce permanent loss inside the roster

Ending image:
A recovered archive points toward a gate leading out of the starter graveyard.

### Book 4 - Gatefall Sector

Pitch:
Crossing into a new sector means harder wrecks, stranger mission rules, and enemies with partial admin authority over the system. Rook is no longer just scavenging for survival; now they are trespassing in someone else's machine.

Book goal:
- scale from local to regional stakes
- upgrade the fleet and crew levels
- reveal that the system can alter mission logic in real time
- introduce the first enemy who weaponizes system law

Ending image:
Rook steals a forbidden command and realizes the system can be edited from inside.

### Book 5 - Stationbreaker Court

Pitch:
Trade networks, alliances, and salvage politics become as deadly as hull breaches when rival station lords force Rook into a court of contracts, hostages, and public spectacle built on resource scarcity.

Book goal:
- make politics as crunchy as combat
- put the crew under internal strain
- show that power growth creates visibility
- give the protagonist their biggest public victory at terrible private cost

Ending image:
The crew keeps the fleet, but the system marks Rook as an active anomaly.

### Book 6 - Cryo God Protocol

Pitch:
Investigating deep-space surveys and hidden labs reveals that the cryo network was built to sort human survivors into usable classes. Rook races to stop a sector reset that would re-freeze or erase everyone they have awakened.

Book goal:
- pay off long-seeded mystery
- force all crew roles to matter at once
- push the system from external pressure to existential threat
- turn the fleet into a rebellion, not just a survival machine

Ending image:
Rook prevents the reset locally but wakes something ancient in the network core.

### Book 7 - The Last Salvage Law

Pitch:
When the system starts collapsing under contradictory commands, every faction in the graveyard rushes either to seize admin control or escape. Rook has to choose between becoming the new custodian of the machine or destroying the laws that kept everyone alive.

Book goal:
- converge crew arcs, faction arcs, and system mystery
- force a final choice between control and freedom
- end the first major saga cleanly while leaving room for deeper sectors or sequel arcs

Ending image:
The graveyard opens, the old rules break, and the survivors step into a larger universe with no guarantee the system is truly gone.

## Length targets based on Dungeon Crawler Carl comps

Official word counts are not readily published, so use published hardcover page counts as the main comparison point and treat the word counts below as rough drafting targets.

| 2 | `Carl's Doomsday Scenario` | 351 | 100k-115k words |
| 3 | `The Dungeon Anarchist's Cookbook` | 532 | 150k-175k words |
| 4 | `The Gate of the Feral Gods` | 586 | 165k-190k words |
| 5 | `The Butcher's Masquerade` | 719 | 205k-230k words |
| 6 | `The Eye of the Bedlam Bride` | 694 | 195k-225k words |
| 7 | `This Inevitable Ruin` | 724 | 205k-235k words |

Practical drafting advice:

- If you want faster release cadence, keep Books 1-3 closer to the low end.
- Let Books 4-7 expand only if the side cast and faction game are carrying their weight.
- Do not bloat early books with lore. Earn the long lengths by widening the map and the crew.

## Book 1 structure recommendation

Use a five-part spine.

### Part I - Wake

- cryo revival
- system reveal
- station triage
- first derelict selected

### Part II - First dives

- first salvage success
- first near-fatal breach
- first evidence other survivors exist

### Part III - First investment

- enough resources to build the first room
- first power cell decision
- first awakened crew member

### Part IV - First war

- rival scavenger interference
- first meaningful ship claim
- the protagonist realizes the system rewards ruthless behavior

### Part V - First expansion

- second ship secured
- crew loyalty tested
- final reveal points to deeper sectors and larger rules

## Why this concept can sustain a long LitRPG run

The progression stack is already layered:

1. survive a run
2. claim a ship
3. choose mine vs keep
4. convert to station
5. build rooms
6. wake crew
7. level crew
8. run missions
9. manage faction pressure
10. uncover system truth

That means every novel can deliver:

- tactical action
- measurable progression
- emotional cast growth
- strategic expansion
- mystery escalation

## Immediate next writing move

If you continue from here, write Book 1 around one clean promise:

`A tired salvage-tech wakes in a lethal orbital system where every life is gated behind hard stats, and the only way to save anyone is to become very, very good at the machine that is killing them.`

Then draft these first three scenes:

1. Cryo wake and status screen.
2. First walk through the starter station and proof the numbers are real.
3. First salvage run ending in either a narrow extract or a hull-breach fakeout.

That opening will tell the reader exactly what kind of LitRPG series this is.
